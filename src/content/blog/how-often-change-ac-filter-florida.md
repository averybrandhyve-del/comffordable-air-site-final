# How Often Should You Change Your AC Filter in Florida?

The box your filter came in says every 60 to 90 days. That recommendation was written for a home in a temperate climate running its AC four to five months a year. In Tampa Bay, your AC runs ten to eleven months a year. That is a fundamentally different situation, and using the same schedule leaves you with a clogged filter, poor airflow, and an HVAC system working harder than it needs to.

Here is the honest answer, based on what we see in homes across the Greater Tampa Bay Area every single day.

## The Florida Reality

Most homes in our service area should be checking their filter every 30 days and replacing it every 45 to 60 days during the summer months. If you have pets, allergies, or live near construction, that window shrinks to every 30 days.

During the "cooler" months (November through February), you can stretch to every 60 days, but your system is still running most days, so don't go longer than that.

## Why Florida Is Different

**Year-round operation.** Your AC in Tampa runs roughly 2.5 times as many hours as an AC in Atlanta. More airflow hours means more particles accumulate on the filter faster.

**High outdoor humidity.** Humidity carries more particulate matter. When humid outdoor air enters your home through gaps and infiltration, it brings with it pollen, mold spores, and dust that all get pulled through your system.

**Pollen seasons overlap.** Florida has multiple overlapping pollen seasons across the year, not one spring bloom. Different trees, grasses, and weeds release pollen across nine months, all of which ends up in your filter.

**Mold spores.** Florida's climate keeps mold spore counts elevated year-round. A clogged filter that can no longer trap these spores effectively means they circulate through your ductwork and settle in your home.

## What Happens If You Wait Too Long

A clogged filter forces your blower motor to work harder to pull air through it. This raises your electric bill, stresses the motor, and reduces airflow over your evaporator coil. Reduced airflow over the coil can cause it to drop below freezing and ice over, which shuts down your cooling entirely.

We see this happen in Tampa homes every summer. A homeowner notices their AC isn't keeping up in August, we arrive and find a filter so restricted it looks like grey carpet. A $15 filter change caused by skipping the schedule results in a service call, a thawed coil, and sometimes blower motor wear.

## Choosing the Right Filter

For most Tampa Bay homes, a Merv-8 to Merv-11 filter is the right balance. Higher Merv ratings (13+) trap more particles but also restrict airflow more, which can stress systems that aren't designed for that resistance.

If you have pets or allergies, aim for Merv-11. If someone in the home has asthma or respiratory conditions, consider our [Indoor Air Quality solutions](/iaq) including whole-home HEPA filtration, which operates separately from your standard filter and provides hospital-grade particle removal.

Do not use fiberglass panel filters (the cheap blue or green ones). They have a Merv rating of 1 to 4 and exist to protect the equipment, not to filter the air you breathe.

## A Simple System That Works

Set a monthly phone reminder on the first of every month to check your filter. Hold it up to a light source. If you can't see light through it clearly, replace it. If it still looks relatively clean, put it back and check again in two weeks.

Buy filters in a six-pack so you always have one on hand. Procrastinating because you need to make a hardware store run is how 90-day filters turn into six-month filters in practice.
