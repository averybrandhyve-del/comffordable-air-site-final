# Why Your AC Isn't Cooling Your Home in Tampa, FL

Running your AC all day in Tampa and still sweating at 77 degrees is one of the most frustrating situations a Florida homeowner faces. The good news: most cases have a specific, diagnosable cause. The bad news: ignoring them turns a $150 repair into a $3,000 compressor replacement.

Here are the five most common reasons your AC runs but won't cool, what each one means, and when you need a professional.

## 1. A Dirty or Clogged Air Filter

This is the single most common cause of reduced cooling in Florida homes, and it costs you nothing to fix. When your filter is packed with dust and debris, airflow drops. Less air moving over the evaporator coil means less heat gets pulled out of your home. On humid Florida days, this can also cause the coil to freeze over, which makes the problem dramatically worse.

Check your filter first. If it's grey and visibly clogged, replace it with a Merv-8 or higher. Filters in Florida should be checked every 30 days during heavy-use months (May through October), not quarterly like most manufacturer guides suggest. Our climate forces far more particulate through the system than northern states.

## 2. Low Refrigerant from a Leak

Your AC doesn't "use up" refrigerant the way a car uses gas. If your system is low, there's a leak somewhere in the system. Low refrigerant means the evaporator coil can't absorb enough heat, so the air coming out of your vents feels warm or barely cooler than the air going in.

Signs of low refrigerant: warm air from vents, ice buildup on the copper lines near the air handler, a hissing or bubbling sound from the unit, and higher-than-normal electric bills. This is not a DIY fix. Refrigerant handling requires EPA 608 certification. A licensed technician needs to find the leak, repair it, and recharge the system.

## 3. A Frozen Evaporator Coil

When airflow drops (dirty filter, closed vents, blocked return) or refrigerant is low, the evaporator coil drops below freezing and a layer of ice forms on it. Ice is a great insulator — it blocks the coil from absorbing heat entirely, so your system blows air but accomplishes nothing.

To confirm: feel the copper line running into your air handler. If it's coated in ice or frost, you have a frozen coil. Turn the system to fan-only (no cooling) for 2 to 4 hours to let it thaw, then replace the filter. If it freezes again after thawing, the underlying cause — low airflow or low refrigerant — hasn't been fixed, and you need a technician.

## 4. A Failing Compressor

The compressor is the engine of your AC system. It pressurizes the refrigerant and drives the entire cooling cycle. A failing compressor often still runs — the system comes on, the outside unit hums — but it doesn't build enough pressure to actually move heat out of your home.

This is the most expensive single component in your system. A compressor replacement runs $1,200 to $2,500 depending on the system. On systems older than 10 years in Florida's climate (which ages equipment faster than most climates), replacement often makes more financial sense than repair. A technician can check compressor pressures in under 20 minutes to tell you definitively which direction to go.

## 5. Duct Leaks Dumping Cold Air Into Your Attic

In Florida homes, ductwork typically runs through unconditioned attic space that can reach 130 degrees in summer. If your ducts have leaks — even small ones at joints and connections — the cold air your system produces is escaping into that heat before it ever reaches your living space.

Signs of duct leaks: some rooms cool well while others don't, unexplained spikes in electric bills, and excessive dust near supply vents. Duct sealing is one of the highest-ROI improvements a Tampa Bay homeowner can make. In some cases, we see 20 to 30 percent efficiency gains after sealing leaky ductwork.
